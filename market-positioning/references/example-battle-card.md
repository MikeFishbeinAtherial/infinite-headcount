# Example Battle Card — Annotated

This is a completed battle card based on a real competitor scrape. Use it as a quality reference.

---

## Completed Battle Card

```
GRAPH ADVISORS — OUTCOMES ENGINEER
Category: Outcomes Engineer
ICP: PE firms, roll-up companies, growth-stage operators, VC firms
Delivery: Embedded — 3-month initial engagement, then expand

WHAT THEY DO WELL
- Strongest anti-legacy-consulting framing of any competitor reviewed
- "Clarity into code" — memorable, ownable, two-word tagline
- Excellent problem framing: "You have a coordination problem, not a technology problem"
- Embedded model is differentiating vs. project-based shops
- "Each solved problem becomes infrastructure" — strong compounding framing
- Philosophy section builds trust without being arrogant

WEAKNESSES / GAPS
- No proof — no case studies, no named clients, no outcomes with numbers
- Vague on what they actually build — no technical specificity anywhere on site
- No team credentialing — who are these people and why should I trust them?
- Copy reads as polished philosophy, not sales — unclear how to engage
- No signal distinguishing custom engineering from off-the-shelf tool deployment

MEMORABLE LINES
- "Clarity into code."
- "You already pay for software. You already store data in the cloud. But none of it connects cleanly."
- "AI transformation without clarity is theater."
- "It compounds. Each solved problem becomes infrastructure. Infrastructure becomes enterprise value."

BADGES: [Embedded] [PE / VC / Growth] [Outcomes-first] [3-month start] [Anti-consulting]
```

---

## Annotation Notes

**Why the weaknesses matter:**
The "no proof" weakness is the most critical gap. At premium price points, buyers need specific evidence. A site that reads as a philosophy statement rather than a sales asset loses immediately to a competitor with strong case studies and specific outcomes.

**The memorable lines section is critical:**
Capture exact language from the competitor's site — even if you're paraphrasing the positioning elsewhere, direct quotes reveal their copywriting quality and what they think their most resonant message is.

**ICP signals often hide in secondary pages:**
Most sites bury ICP detail. Look for it in: service page subheadings, case study pages, "who we work with" sections, or FAQ answers about company size.

**Delivery model often isn't named explicitly:**
You'll infer it from words like "embed," "sprint," "retainer," "pilot," or "engagement." Note what they say about timeline and scope.

## What a Weak Battle Card Looks Like (Avoid This)

```
COMPETITOR X
They are a company that helps businesses with their marketing.

STRENGTHS
- Good website
- Experienced team

WEAKNESSES
- Expensive
- Not very specific
```

This is useless. A good battle card has:
- Specific language pulled from the site
- Concrete ICP (not just "businesses" or "enterprises")
- Real weaknesses with strategic implications
- Memorable lines verbatim
- Badges that summarize positioning at a glance
