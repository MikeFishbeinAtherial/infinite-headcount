# Homepage Copy Examples — Section by Section

## The Most Important Section: The Hero

The hero has one job: make the right person feel like they've found exactly what they were looking for. It should not try to explain everything.

### Strong Hero Examples

**Service Company — Strategy + Execution**
> We show you what's possible. Then we build it.
>
> [Company] is a strategy and engineering partner for founder-led companies. We identify where [technology] can transform your business, build a clear roadmap, and engineer custom systems that create new capabilities — then stay as your partner as the work compounds.

Why it works: The headline leads with the core insight. The subheadline explains who it's for and what they get without over-explaining the process.

**Product / SaaS**
> The system your team has been waiting for.
>
> [Product] eliminates manual work for ops-heavy teams — connecting fragmented tools, automating repetitive processes, and creating visibility into what's actually happening in your business.

Why it works: Leads with outcome (the system they've been waiting for), immediately grounds it in what's being built.

## Problem Section: How to Frame Pain Without Being Negative

Strong problem sections:
1. Validate the reader (you've tried the obvious things)
2. Name the root cause (not the symptom)
3. Explain who's to blame (the incumbent or the category, not the reader)

**Weak:**
> [Category] is transforming every industry. Companies that don't adapt will be left behind.

This is fear-based, generic, and reads as a vendor talking down to a prospect.

**Strong:**
> You know this is important. You've tried the tools. Maybe you've run a pilot.
>
> But nothing has fundamentally changed yet. Not because you lack the will — because no one has shown you what's actually possible in the context of how your business works. And when they did show you something, they couldn't execute it.

This validates the reader, blames the gap on incumbents, and creates urgency without fear.

## Pain Point Callouts: Formula

Each callout = [bold label] + [1–2 sentence explanation]

**Formula:**
> **[The root cause, named directly.]** [Sentence explaining why this happens and what the cost is.]

**Examples:**
> **You don't know what to build.** The options are endless. Most companies can't see clearly what would actually move the needle for their specific business — so they experiment without a real strategy.

> **Strategy and execution are owned by different people.** Consultants can't build. Vendors need you to tell them what to build. The gap between insight and shipped result is where most initiatives die.

> **This is a specialized skillset.** Building systems that handle complex, real-world workflows requires deep expertise — not the kind you can hire for in a few months.

## Service Descriptions: Lead with the Problem Each Service Solves

**Weak:**
> **Strategy & Roadmapping**
> We offer comprehensive strategy services including discovery workshops, stakeholder interviews, and roadmap development.

Process-focused. Reads like a brochure.

**Strong:**
> **Strategy & Roadmapping**
> Before we do anything else, we get clear on what to build and why. We embed with your team, map your operations, and identify the highest-leverage opportunities specific to your business. You walk away with a concrete, prioritized roadmap — not a deck of generic possibilities.

Leads with the problem (building the wrong thing), explains the process, ends with the outcome.

## How It Works: The Process Arc

Discovery → Strategy → Build → Expand (or your version of this)

Every premium engagement follows a similar arc. The key is naming what makes each step differentiated — not just listing generic phase names.

- **Discovery** isn't just "a kickoff call" — it's deep research most clients are surprised by
- **Strategy** isn't just a document — it's specific, prioritized, executable
- **Build** isn't a prototype — it's a working system that handles real-world complexity
- **Expand** isn't a vague promise — it's the compounding value narrative

## Who It's For: The Self-Selection Section

This section does two jobs:
1. Makes the right prospect lean in and say "that's me"
2. Quietly filters out the wrong prospects

**Three self-selection signals formula:**
Each signal = one sentence describing a scenario the right buyer recognizes in themselves.

> You've tried the off-the-shelf options and feel like you're barely scratching the surface.
> You've been pitched by consultants who gave you a strategy and vendors who needed you to spec it — and neither worked.
> You're ready to move from curious to capable, and you want a team that does both the thinking and the building.

**Engagement or pricing qualifier (signal without a price card):**
> We take on a small number of clients at a time. Typical engagements run [range]. We work selectively because depth requires it.

This line:
- Creates scarcity
- Signals the price range (filters wrong-fit leads)
- Explains the exclusivity with a reason that's about quality, not arrogance

## Team Section: Credibility Without Ego

**Wrong:**
> Our team of world-class experts has decades of combined experience delivering cutting-edge solutions.

Generic, unverifiable, and everyone says this.

**Right:**
> Most [category] shops arrived from adjacent fields twelve months ago. We've been doing this since before it was obvious.
>
> [Company] is [team description]. We don't staff junior talent and charge a premium for our names. When you work with us, you work with us.

Names the shared enemy (people who arrived recently), makes a specific claim (not junior), ends with a trust statement.

**Bios: two sentences max**
- Sentence 1: tenure + domain
- Sentence 2: what they're responsible for on this team

---

## CTA: Mirror the Headline

The closing CTA should echo the hero. If the hero is "We show you what's possible. Then we build it." — the CTA should be "Let's show you what's possible."

The supporting line should remove friction:
> No commitment. No sales deck. A direct conversation about what's possible for your business.
